I am a research scientist at the Helmholtz Institute for Human-Centered AI, working with Dr. Eric Schulz.
My research is focused on using large language models (LLMs) for hypothesis generation.
I obtained my PhD at UC Berkeley (2024) and my undergraduate degree at Brown University (2018).

Computational cognitive modeling

My research centers on developing computational models that translate cognitive theories into concise algorithms, with parameters that map onto cognitive mechanisms impacting observable behavior. This work involves quantifying individual differences in information processing through model parameters, pinpointing the most suitable theories embedded within various cognitive models, and investigating the interactions among cognitive processes like executive function, working memory, and reinforcement learning.

Model fitting tools

Cognitive science researchers heavily rely on tools to connect computational cognitive models with data, often through likelihood estimation. However, numerous models do not have tractable likelihood functions, thus limiting the range of testable theories. My work has also focused on implementing an alternative model-fitting approach using simple, light-weight artificial neural networks that enables parameter estimation and model identification solely based on data sequences, while circumventing the need for likelihood estimation.

Published work:

Large language models

I recently started working with large language models. I am broadly interested in their ability to reason through data sets of human behavior from cognitive tasks, and generate plausible hypotheses with regards to underlying mechanisms that explain the data.
